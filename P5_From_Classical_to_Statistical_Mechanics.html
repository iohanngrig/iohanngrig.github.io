From Classical Mechanics to Statistical Mechanics¶

${\bf \large \cal Hovhannes \ Grigoryan}\\ {\rm \small New \ York, \ NY}$

Abstract

We describe the idea behind the event-driven molecular dynamics algorithm. We use this algorithm to create a simulation with hard disks, and study how the velocity distribution of one the disks approach Maxwell's distribution. Then we discuss statistical ensembles, and how to sample legitimate configurations using Monte Carlo sampling for hard-wall boxes, as well as boxes with periodic boundary conditions. We also describe the Markov-chain Monte Carlo algorithm to generate valid configurations [1]. At the end we discuss the 'derivation' of Maxwell/Boltzmann distribution using equiprobability, and basic geometric considerations [2].

Content

  • Event-Driven Molecular Dynamics Algorithm
  • Molecular Dynamics Simulation in 2d with N hard disks
  • Statistical Ensemble (Monte Carlo Sampling)
  • Box with periodic boundary conditions
  • Markov-chain Monte Carlo for hard disks
  • Appendix: Maxwell/Boltzmann Distribution [2]

Alder and Wainwright's Event-Driven Molecular Dynamics Algorithm¶

The algorithm is called event-driven, since it moves forward in a sequence of collisions, called events. The proportion of images where two disks are in contact with each other, or one disk is in contact with a wall is zero. There are no collisions between three disks, or a collision of two disks taking place exactly at the same time as a wall collision.

The disks of radius $r$ move inside the square $R = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 ~|~ x,y \in [0,1]\}$. There are two types of collisions: disk-wall and disk-disk collisions. The wall_time function below calculates time required for a disk to hit the closest wall along one spacial dimension, if it starts at coordinate $x_0$ and velocity $v_x$.

The logic is straightforward: if $v_x>0$, the disk will collide the right wall after time determined from equation

$$x_0 + v_x dt = 1 - r.$$

On the other hand, if $v_x<0$, the disk will collide the left wall, and the corresponding time is determined from:

$$x_0 - |v_x| dt = r.$$

Finally, in case, $v_x=0$, time to reach either of the walls is infinite.

Similarly, the pair_time function determines the time of disk-disk collision, given initial position and velocity vectors. In this case, assume disk $A$ is at rest in the center of the coordinate system. Let $d\vec{x}=d\vec{x}_B - d\vec{x}_A$ and $d\vec{v}=d\vec{v}_B - d\vec{v}_A$ be relative position and velocity. Then, collision of disk $A$ with disk $B$ is possible if

$$d\vec{v}d\vec{x} = dvdx \cos(\beta) < 0 ,$$

which means that relative velocity points towards the half of the plane where the other disk is. However, to hit the other disk, $\beta$ should be larger than $\arcsin(2r/dx)$. In all other cases, the disks will never collide. The collision time is determined from the quadratic equation:

$$dv^2 dt^2 + 2(d\vec{v}d\vec{x})dt + dx^2 - 4r^2 = 0,$$

which follows from the triangle formed by $2r$, $d\vec{v}dt$ and $d\vec{x}$.

def wall_time(x_a, v_a, r):
    """ Time to collide with a wall in x_a direction. 
        x_a - initial coordinate (of disk center) along a-axis
        v_a - velocity at that point
        r - disk radius
    """
    if v_a > 0.0:
        dt = (1.0 - r - x_a) / v_a
    elif v_a < 0.0:
        dt = (x_a - r) / abs(v_a)
    else:
        dt = np.inf
    return dt

def pair_time(x_A, v_A, x_B, v_B, r):
    """ Time of A-B disk-disk collision.
        x_A/B = (x_1, x_2), v_A/B = (v_1, v_2) vectors of initial 
        (center) position for disk A/B and their velocities
        r - disk radius
    """
    dx, dv = x_B - x_A, v_B - v_A
    dxdx, dvdv, dvdx = np.dot(dx, dx), np.dot(dv, dv), np.dot(dv, dx)
    hit = dvdx ** 2 - dvdv * (dxdx - 4.0 * r ** 2)
    if hit > 0.0 and dvdx < 0.0:
        dt = - (dvdx + np.sqrt(hit)) / dvdv
    else:
        dt = np.inf
    return dt

def min_arg(l):
    return min(zip(l, range(len(l))))

def compute_next_event(pos, vel, r, singles, pairs):
    """ Finds the event that requires the shortest time.
        Params: pos - vectors of positions, vel - vectors of velocities, r - radius of a disk,
        Returns: the argument of the event
    """
    wall_times = [wall_time(pos[k][l], vel[k][l], r) for k, l in singles] # k = ball_A, ball_B, ..., l = x, y
    pair_times = [pair_time(pos[k], vel[k], pos[l], vel[l], r) for k, l in pairs] # k, l = ball_A, ball_B, ...
    return min_arg(wall_times + pair_times)

def compute_new_velocities(pos, vel, next_event_arg, singles, pairs):
    if next_event_arg < len(singles):
        # the disk hits the wall, and velocity changes direction
        collision_disk, coord_dir = singles[next_event_arg]
        vel[collision_disk][coord_dir] *= -1.0
    else:
        # two disks collide, and change their velocities
        a, b = pairs[next_event_arg - len(singles)]
        dx, dv = pos[b]- pos[a], vel[b] - vel[a]
        e_perp = dx/np.linalg.norm(dx)
        scal = np.dot(dv, e_perp)
        for k in range(2):
            vel[a][k] += e_perp[k] * scal
            vel[b][k] -= e_perp[k] * scal
            
def get_temperature(vel):
    "Get's T from initial velocities, in units where k_B = 1 and m = 1."
    N = len(vel)
    d = 2
    vel_x, vel_y = vel[:, 0], vel[:, 1]
    Ekin = 0.5 * (np.dot(vel_x, vel_x) + np.dot(vel_y, vel_y))
    e_pdf = Ekin/(d*N)
    T = 2 * e_pdf
    return T

def direct_disks_box(N, sigma):
    "Creates valid disc configuration."
    condition = False
    while condition == False:
        L = [[random.uniform(sigma, 1.0 - sigma), random.uniform(sigma, 1.0 - sigma)]]
        for k in range(1, N):
            a = [random.uniform(sigma, 1.0 - sigma), random.uniform(sigma, 1.0 - sigma)]
            min_dist = min(np.sqrt((a[0] - b[0]) ** 2 + (a[1] - b[1]) ** 2) for b in L)
            if min_dist < 2.0 * sigma:
                condition = False
                break
            else:
                L.append(a)
                condition = True
    return np.array(L)

def dist(x, y):
    d_x = abs(x[0] - y[0]) % 1.0
    d_x = min(d_x, 1.0 - d_x)
    d_y = abs(x[1] - y[1]) % 1.0
    d_y = min(d_y, 1.0 - d_y)
    return np.sqrt(d_x ** 2 + d_y ** 2)

def direct_disks(N, sigma):
    n_iter = 0
    condition = False
    while condition == False:
        n_iter += 1
        L = [(random.random(), random.random())]
        for k in range(1, N):
            a = (random.random(), random.random())
            min_dist = min(dist(a, b) for b in L)
            if min_dist < 2.0 * sigma:
                condition = False
                break
            else:
                L.append(a)
                condition = True
    return n_iter, L

def periodicize(config):
    images = [-1.0, 0.0, 1.0]
    return [(x + dx, y + dy) for (x, y) in config for dx in images for dy in images]

Maxwell Distribution¶

Consider classical ideal gas with $N$ particles and total energy $E$ in $d$ spacial dimensions. Then, for an isolated system,

$$ \sum_{i=1}^N \vec{v}^2_i = 2 E/m $$

where $m$ is the mass of a single particle, $\vec{v}_i$ is the velocity vector of the $i$-th particle. Therefore all accessible velocity (energy) states live on a $(dN-1)$-sphere of radius $R=\sqrt{2E/m}$ with surface area:

$$ A_{dN-1}(R)= \frac{2\pi^{dN/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{dN}{2}\right)}R^{dN-1} \sim \sqrt{\frac{d}{\pi}}\left(\frac{2\pi e}{dN}\right)^{dN/2}R^{dN-1} $$

Assume that each point on the $(dN-1)$-sphere is equiprobable, therefore the probability $\pi(v_{ik}) dv_{ik}$ of finding the $k$-th component of $i$-th particle velocity, with normalization $\int \pi(v_{ik})dv_{ik} = 1$ is proportional to

$$ \pi(v_{ik}) = A_{dN-2}\left(\sqrt{2E/m - v_{ik}^2}\right)/A_{dN-1}\left(\sqrt{2E/m}\right) \sim \sqrt{\frac{m}{4\pi \epsilon}} \exp\left\{-\frac{m v^2_{ik}}{4 \epsilon}\right\}$$

where $\epsilon = E/(dN)$ is energy per degree of freedom. Similar ideas were discussed in Ref.[2]. Taking into account that entropy is

$$S=-kdN \int \pi(x)\ln \pi(x) dx \sim \frac{1}{2}kdN \ln\frac{E}{N} ,$$

and $\left(\partial S/\partial E\right)_N = 1/T$, it follows that $\frac{1}{2}kT = E/dN = \epsilon$.

When particle interaction is not just through the collisions, $E$ is the maximal energy, and

$$ \sum_{i=1}^N \vec{v}^2_i \leq 2 E/m $$

However, since $V_{dN}/A_{dN-1} = R/dN$, the volume of a ball is "concentrated around its boundary", because

$$\frac{\text{Volume of outermost shell}}{\text{Total Volume}} = \frac{V_{dN}(R) - V_{dN}(R-\delta R)}{V_{dN}(R)} \approx dN \frac{\delta R}{R},$$

and this ratio is of order one if $\delta R \sim R/(dN)$. Therefore, the above analysis that led to Maxwell distribution is still valid. In our case, $R = \sqrt{2E/m}$, therefore, $\delta E \sim 2E/(dN) \sim k T$.

To summarize:

  • Maxwell distribution for one component of velocity in d space dimensions, can be written as:
$$\pi(v_x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-v^2_x/2\sigma^2}$$
  • Since the mean energy per degree of freedom is: $E/dN = kT/2 \ \implies \ \sigma^2 = \frac{2E}{mdN} = kT/m$.
  • Velocity distribution of a statistical ensemble can be, in principle, obtained by sampling a random point on the surface of a hypersphere. However, the dimension of this hypersphere should be comparable to the Avogadro number. This is why Maxwell distribution is helpful.
  • To relate Molecular Dynamics and Markov-chain Monte Carlo methods, we can sample velocities in Markov-chain method using Maxwell's distribution.

Boltzmann Distribution¶

Consider a system that is made up of parts with energies $E_i$, where $i=1 \dots N$, such that $\sum_{i=1}^N E_i \leq E$. In this case, the states of different energies lie within a simplex of volume

$$V_{N}(E) = E^N/N!$$

Assuming that all these states are equiprobable and that probability density function, $\pi(E_i)$ is normalized as $\int^E_0 \pi(E_i)dE_i$, we will get:

$$ \pi(E_i) = V_{N-1}(E-E_i)/V_N(E) \sim \frac{1}{\epsilon} \exp\left\{-E_i/\epsilon\right\} $$

where $\epsilon = E/N$. As in the case with Maxwell distribution, the volume of the simplex is concentrated near its boundary, since ratio of boundary slice of thickness $\delta E$ to the total volume is: $\sim N \delta E/E $. To express $\epsilon$ through the temperature, we calculate the entropy, and find its partial derivative w.r.t. energy, as before. In this case, $kT = E/N = \epsilon$, and $\pi(E_i) \sim \exp\left\{-E_i/kT\right\}$.
